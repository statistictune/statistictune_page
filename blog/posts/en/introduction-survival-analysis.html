May 28, 2026 · Rafael Aguilar Magalhães · 8 min read

Introduction to Survival Analysis:
from concept to Kaplan-Meier

What is censoring, why time-to-event data requires special methods, and how to interpret a Kaplan-Meier curve step by step - from zero to a publication-ready plot in R.

Also available in: PT - Português EN - English

Why special methods?

In clinical studies, the outcome of interest is frequently the time until an event - death, relapse, hospitalization, disease progression. The central problem: not all participants experience the event during the follow-up period. Some drop out voluntarily, others are lost to follow-up, and others simply haven't reached the event by the time data collection ends.

This phenomenon is called censoring. Ignoring censored patients would waste valuable information - we know they survived at least until the censoring time. Treating them as if they had experienced the event would introduce serious bias. Survival analysis methods exist precisely to handle this situation.

Formal definition: Right censoring occurs when an individual's follow-up time ends before the event of interest is observed. The observed value is a lower bound for the true time to event.

The survival function

The central object of survival analysis is the survival function S(t), representing the probability that an individual has not yet experienced the event by time t:

S(t) = P(T > t) = 1 − F(t)
T = random variable representing time to event; F(t) = cumulative distribution function; S(0) = 1 and S(∞) → 0.

S(t) is a monotonically decreasing function starting at 1 (nobody experienced the event at time zero) and tending toward zero as time progresses. In practice, with real data and censoring, we need to estimate this function - and that's where Kaplan-Meier comes in.

The Kaplan-Meier estimator

Proposed by Edward Kaplan and Paul Meier in 1958, the KM estimator is the standard non-parametric method for estimating S(t) from censored data. The key idea is to compute the survival probability as a product of conditional probabilities, updating the estimate only at times when events occur:

Ŝ(t) = ∏ (1 − dᵢ / nᵢ) t(i) ≤ t
dᵢ = number of events at time t(i); nᵢ = number of individuals at risk immediately before t(i) (includes censored individuals still under follow-up).

The elegance of the estimator lies in how it handles censoring: censored individuals contribute to the denominator (they were at risk) until they exit, but not to the numerator (they didn't experience the event). This preserves all available information without making assumptions about what would have happened after censoring.

A simple numerical example

Consider 5 patients with the following follow-up times (+ = censored):

2, 3+, 5, 6+, 8

Events occur at times 2, 5, and 8. The KM calculation:

  • t = 2: 1 event, 5 at risk → S(2) = (1 − 1/5) = 0.80
  • t = 5: 1 event, 3 at risk (censored patient at t=3 already exited) → S(5) = 0.80 × (1 − 1/3) ≈ 0.53
  • t = 8: 1 event, 1 at risk → S(8) = 0.53 × (1 − 1/1) = 0.00

Implementation in R

The survival package is the standard for survival analysis in R. For visualization, survminer provides publication-ready curves with an integrated risk table: 

install.packages(c("survival", "survminer"))

library(survival)
library(survminer)

# Fit KM curve stratified by group
km_fit <- survfit(Surv(time, status) ~ group, data = your_dataset)

# Summary with median survival and 95% CI
summary(km_fit)

# Publication-ready visualization
ggsurvplot(
  km_fit,
  data        = your_dataset,
  pval        = TRUE,          # log-rank p-value
  conf.int    = TRUE,          # 95% confidence interval
  risk.table  = TRUE,          # number at risk table
  palette     = c("#1B6B7B", "#C9A84C"),
  xlab        = "Time (days)",
  ylab        = "Survival probability",
  legend.labs = c("Group A", "Group B"),
  ggtheme     = theme_minimal()
)

How to read the curve

When interpreting a Kaplan-Meier curve, pay attention to four elements:

  • Median survival: the time at which S(t) = 0.5 - half the individuals have not yet experienced the event. Preferred over the mean because it is robust to censoring.
  • Confidence interval: the bands around the curve. They widen over time because fewer individuals remain at risk - estimates become less precise in the tail.
  • Risk table: shows how many individuals were still being followed at each time point. Essential for evaluating the reliability of tail estimates.
  • Log-rank test: compares curves between groups. Tests H₀: S₁(t) = S₂(t) for all t. Assumes no parametric form, but is most sensitive to proportional differences (proportional hazards).
Practical note: When the risk table shows few individuals (n < 5) in the tail of the curve, S(t) estimates in that region are highly imprecise. Avoid interpreting or comparing curves there - consider truncating the x-axis at a time point where a reasonable number of individuals are still at risk.

Next steps

Kaplan-Meier is descriptive - it estimates and visualizes survival, but does not adjust for covariates. For modeling with multiple predictors, the natural next step is the Cox model, which estimates adjusted hazard ratios while preserving the non-parametric baseline survival function.

When more than one type of event is competing (e.g., cause-specific death vs. other causes), the Fine-Gray competing risks model is the correct approach - the KM estimator overestimates cumulative incidence in those scenarios.

Good survival analysis starts at data collection: defining time zero, the event of interest, and the censoring criterion with surgical precision - before opening the dataset.
